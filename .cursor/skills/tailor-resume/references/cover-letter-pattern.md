# Cover letter pattern

Write `cover-letter.md` as a short, conversational note to a hiring manager — closer to a thoughtful email than a formal business letter. Every claim must trace to `resume.md`.

## Tone

Friendly, peer-to-peer, and grounded. It should read like an engineer reaching out about a role they're genuinely interested in, not like a templated application packet.

- Plain language. Contractions are fine ("I've", "I'd").
- Short sentences. No marketing voice, no résumé restatement.
- The reader is a human; address them like one.

## Shape

- 2–3 short paragraphs, roughly 3 lines each (~180–280 words total).
- One opening, one body, one close — or split the body in two if there's a clear bridge moment.
- A relaxed greeting (`Hi there,` or `Hi [Name],` when the per-run prompt provides a name).
- A relaxed sign-off (`Thanks,` or `Best,`) followed by the candidate's first name.
- No formal letterhead block, no date line, no recipient address block. Contact details live in `resume.md`; the cover letter is just the note.

## What each paragraph does

1. **Opening (~3 lines)** — Name the role, then go straight to the strongest verified resume fact for the job. Skip throat-clearing about why-this-company unless there's a real reason that isn't generic.
2. **Body (~3 lines, optionally split in two)** — The most relevant adjacent experience from `resume.md`. Stay on verified strengths; do not list what is missing or pre-emptively flag gaps.
3. **Close (~3 lines)** — One concrete topic the user would want to understand on a first call, plus a friendly sign-off line.

## Sourcing rules

- Only `resume.md` facts. Nothing from `suggested-additions.md` may appear as if true.
- Do not flag, name, or apologise for missing specialties in the letter. Gaps belong in `gaps.md` (and inform the interview), not the cover letter. Lean on the verified bridges from `gaps.md` instead.
- Tailor the opening and body to the specific job from the per-run prompt — a generic letter is a failure.

## Avoid

- "I am uniquely qualified" / "perfect match" / "perfect fit"
- "passionate about" / "proven track record"
- "I would like to express my interest in…" and other formal-letter openers
- "Dear Hiring Manager," / "Sincerely," / "Yours truly," — these signal the wrong register
- Re-listing résumé bullets one by one
- Naming or flagging missing specialties ("I haven't shipped X yet…", "no direct experience with Y") — leave that out of the letter entirely
- Apologetic or defensive language
- Emojis, exclamation points, marketing-style superlatives
